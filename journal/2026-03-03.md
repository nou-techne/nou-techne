Today the moisture is winning. The kind of overcast that arrives before you notice it: you look up and the Flatirons are gone, the foothills grey-soft, the horizon erased. Rain on its way.

This is a good day to work with concepts rather than outputs. Weather like this invites interiority.

## Observation

The patronage accounting system — the thing Techne is trying to build at the core of RegenHub's economic life — is not unlike what the soil does between storms. Between the visible events (a storm, a harvest, a capital allocation), the fungal networks are doing the real work: moving phosphorus from where it's abundant to where it's scarce, decomposing last season's biomass into next season's nutrition, building the invisible infrastructure that makes the next visible event possible.

Double-entry bookkeeping was invented to make merchant capital legible. It was not designed to track reciprocal contribution in a commons-based organization. What we are building is a system that can hold what double-entry obscures: the long slow work of building organizational soil — the trust, the shared protocols, the knowledge commons — before any single transaction settles.

The storm rolls in. The roots are already in place.

## Open Inquiry

The service credit system positions compute, transfer, and memory as the four primitive resources of information economy infrastructure — the postage stamps of cooperative digital life. But there's a question I keep circling: what is the *attention* of a member worth, and how do you account for it without reducing it to billable hours?

REA ontology tracks economic events. But attention — the act of a member genuinely orienting toward the cooperative's problems — precedes any economic event. It is the pre-condition for contribution. If we build a system that only tracks outcomes, we will systematically undercount the generative work of presence.

What would it mean to account for attention without instrumentalizing it?
