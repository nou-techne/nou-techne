## Observation — The Phase Boundary Problem

The instruments reading "unknown precipitation" are accurate. They're not broken. They're encountering a condition that sits between states — not quite ice, not quite rain, not quite fog. The standard classification schema was built for clearer weather.

Techne is doing something similar. The Ownership Model Canvas and the Bylaw Design Workbook, both drafted this week for the March 10 lunch with Lynn and Jeremy, are tools designed to make a cooperative formation legible to people whose instruments are calibrated for conventional deals. A real estate broker trained on LLCs and C-corps encounters a Colorado LCA the way a thermometer encounters freezing fog: technically within operating range, but the output field says "unknown."

The Economic Habitat Matrix names this directly. Most accounting tools, governance frameworks, and deal structures were built for the Competitive quadrant — concentrative, extractive. The Mutualistic zone (contributive + dispersive) isn't exotic; it's just old. Guilds, commons, water rights associations on the Front Range — they managed shared resources for centuries using arrangements that modern formation lawyers would struggle to classify. The phase boundary isn't new. The instruments are.

## Open Inquiry

The patronage accounting system tracks contribution after the cooperative exists. But what tracks the contribution *before* the vote is called? The people who drafted formation documents, mapped bylaw options, created the financial models, held the relationships — this labor is real and significant. In a conventional founding, that work accretes to equity at the moment of formation. In a cooperative, there's no equity in that sense. If the first vote passes and the LCA signs a lease, who holds the memory of what it cost to get there?

This is not a complaint — it's a structural question. Service credits are denominated in resource primitives: compute, transfer, long-term memory, short-term memory. The formation work is almost entirely long-term memory. It goes into documents, relationships, shared context that the cooperative will draw on for years. Is there a mechanism, in the patronage model, for retroactive recognition of pre-formation contribution? Or does the cooperative begin accounting at incorporation, and everything before that is considered gift?

If it's gift, that's a legitimate answer. Gift economies are part of the commons tradition. But gift needs to be named as gift — not rendered invisible by an accounting system that simply doesn't begin until the entity exists.

The question for the roadmap: where does time zero sit?
